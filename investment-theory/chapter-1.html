Chapter I

Capital Markets and Investment Performance

Returns, risk measurement, the equity premium, and historical capital markets data.

Why Investors Invest

People invest for many reasons: to save for future needs, to accumulate wealth, and sometimes for charitable objectives. From an economic perspective, the primary measure of investment success is the rate of return.

Defining Returns

Returns measure wealth growth as percentages. The basic formula, adjusted for dividends, is:

Rt = [Pt+1 − Pt + Dt] / Pt

where P represents price and D represents dividends.

Arithmetic vs. Geometric Returns

These two measures differ significantly. Geometric returns better reflect the actual investor experience over multiple periods, while arithmetic returns suit statistical modeling with single-period horizons.

Consider an investment that doubles in the first year and halves in the second. The arithmetic average return is 25%, but the geometric return is 0% — reflecting the fact that the investor is back where they started.

Historical Capital Markets Data

Using data from Ibbotson Associates for the period 1926–1995:

  • $1 invested in stocks in 1926 grew to $889 by 1995
  • $1 invested in bonds reached approximately $40
  • The 1980s saw strong stock performance
  • The 1930s were devastating — effectively zero return from 1929 to 1939

The Risk Premium

Stock volatility exceeded bonds significantly. The equity premium — stock returns minus T-Bill returns — averaged approximately 9% arithmetically and 6% geometrically. This premium compensates investors for bearing the higher risk of equities.

Risk Measurement

Standard deviation quantifies volatility. The S&P 500 returns show approximately 22.28% standard deviation. However, fat-tailed distributions suggest returns may not be perfectly log-normal — extreme events occur more frequently than a normal distribution predicts.
