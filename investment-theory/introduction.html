Introduction

An Introduction to Investment Theory

Finance in five pictures — the figures that carry the logic of financial reasoning.

This book is written for visual thinkers. Three decades of teaching finance have persuaded me that most of financial theory rests on intuitions a handful of simple pictures can carry. Learn to read five of them — really read them — and you will hold the working core of modern financial reasoning.

The claim of this book is deliberately strong: if you understand these five figures, together with the logic and the assumptions beneath them, you will have a working knowledge of investment theory. They are more than illustrations. They are models you can use yourself — to plan, to price, to hedge, to decide — and they are empowering even for the reader who never touches the mathematics.

The Five Pictures

Picture 1 Compound Growth The growth of a dollar over time — the engine of long-horizon investing, and an idea older than money itself. Chapter I → Picture 2 Risk & Return The efficient frontier — how combining risky assets creates possibilities no single asset offers. The only free lunch in finance. Chapter II → Picture 3 The Price of Risk The Security Market Line — how the market rewards only the risk you cannot diversify away, setting the return every asset must earn. Chapters IV–V → Picture 4 The Payoff The option diagram — the hockey stick. How contingent claims reshape outcomes, and why optionality is everywhere once you see it. Chapter IX → Picture 5 Bond Duration Price against the interest rate — how the value of a fixed promise moves when rates move, and how to measure and manage that sensitivity. Chapter XI →

How to Read Each Picture

Every figure in this book is examined through the same four lenses. The first three stand on their own; the fourth is there for those who want it.

1. The logic

What the picture says, in plain terms, before any algebra — the idea it captures and why it matters.

2. How it moves

How its shape and key points shift as you change the assumptions and inputs. The interactive figures let you push on them yourself.

3. How you use it

The part too often skipped — how a practitioner actually deploys the figure to make a decision: to size a position, price a security, hedge an exposure, set a discount rate.

4. The math (optional)

The theory and derivation underneath, boxed off for the mathematically inclined. Skip it freely; the picture still works without it.

Key Idea

These are not five isolated topics. They recur — as motifs, as tools, and as ways of seeing — throughout the chapters that follow. Master the five pictures and you will not have memorized finance; you will be able to reason with it.

A closing invitation, carried through the whole book: play with each picture. The most useful insights come from posing your own questions — when, how, and why does this figure change?
