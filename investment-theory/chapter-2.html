Chapter II

The Geography of the Efficient Frontier

How portfolio construction and diversification create optimal investment opportunities through Modern Portfolio Theory.

Markowitz's Revolutionary Insight

Harry Markowitz transformed investment analysis by proposing that the relevant information about securities can be summarized by three measures: the mean return, the standard deviation of returns, and the correlation with other assets' returns.

Risk-Return Characteristics

Using historical data (1970–3/1995), six asset classes can be plotted by annual standard deviation and average returns: small stocks (highest return, highest risk), S&P stocks, corporate bonds, government bonds, T-Bills (lowest risk), and MSCI international stocks. No single asset dominates for all investors.

Correlation and Diversification

The portfolio standard deviation depends critically on the correlation between assets:

  • Zero correlation: A portfolio mixing 80% Security A with 20% Security B reduces volatility despite B being riskier individually
  • Perfect correlation (+1): No diversification benefit; portfolio risk is a linear combination
  • Negative correlation (−1): Extreme hedging potential, nearly eliminating risk

A key discovery: after approximately 30 stocks with equal weights and zero correlation, diversification benefits plateau significantly.

The Efficient Frontier

The efficient frontier is the set of portfolios offering maximum return for each risk level, or equivalently, minimum risk for each return level. It extends from the maximum-return portfolio to the minimum-variance portfolio, forming the boundary of feasible asset combinations.

Markowitz created the first efficient frontier using NYSE stocks, published in Portfolio Selection (Yale University Press, 1959).

The Capital Market Line

When combining risky assets with risk-free Treasury Bills, the new efficient frontier becomes a ray extending from the risk-free rate, tangentially touching the risky-asset frontier at point M. Portfolios between Rf and M combine T-Bills with risky portfolio M; portfolios beyond M involve borrowing at the risk-free rate to leverage investment in M.

Limitations

The model's critical weakness is its reliance on accurate statistical inputs. Historical mean returns may poorly predict future performance, and correlation estimation errors multiply with more securities. The model works best for asset allocation across broad classes rather than individual stock selection.
