Summary Review Notes

Condensed review of all chapters -- key concepts, equations, and takeaways for exam preparation.

Chapter I: Capital Markets and Investment Performance

Core Topics

Geometric vs. arithmetic returns -- Geometric returns reflect actual compound growth; arithmetic returns are useful for statistical modeling. Historical capital market data (Ibbotson Associates, 1926-1995). The equity premium averaged approximately 8.5% (arithmetic). Standard deviation as the primary measure of volatility.

Basic Return Formula

$$R_t = \frac{P_{t+1} - P_t + D_t}{P_t}$$

Chapter II: The Efficient Frontier

Core Topics

Risk and return -- the fundamental trade-off. Correlation between assets determines diversification benefits. The efficient frontier is the set of portfolios offering maximum return for each level of risk. Adding a riskless asset creates the Capital Allocation Line.

Portfolio Variance (Two Assets)

$$\sigma_p^2 = w_1^2\sigma_1^2 + w_2^2\sigma_2^2 + 2w_1 w_2 \rho_{12}\sigma_1\sigma_2$$

Chapter III: Investor Preferences and the Tangency Portfolio

Core Topics

Iso-utility curves represent investor preferences over risk and return. The shortfall approach and Sharpe ratio provide practical criteria for portfolio selection. The tangency portfolio is the optimal risky portfolio -- the point where the Capital Allocation Line is tangent to the efficient frontier.

Sharpe Ratio

$$S = \frac{E[R_p] - R_f}{\sigma_p}$$

Chapter IV: The Capital Asset Pricing Model

Core Topics

The tangency portfolio equals the market portfolio in equilibrium (all investors hold the same risky portfolio). The CAPM equilibrium: expected return is a linear function of beta. The "Birthday Cake" proof demonstrates that the market portfolio must be the tangency portfolio when all investors hold mean-variance optimal portfolios.

The CAPM

$$E[R_i] = R_f + \beta_i\left(E[R_m] - R_f\right)$$

Chapter V: Further Explorations of the CAPM

Core Topics

Correlations drive attractiveness of a stock -- low correlation with the market portfolio means the asset shifts the frontier favorably. The Security Market Line plots expected return against beta. Beta is the sole risk measure: $\beta = \text{cov}(R_i, R_m)/\text{var}(R_m)$. Only systematic risk is priced.

Beta

$$\beta_i = \frac{\text{cov}(R_i, R_m)}{\text{var}(R_m)} = \frac{\rho_{i,m}\,\sigma_i}{\sigma_m}$$

Chapter VI: The Arbitrage Pricing Theory

Core Topics

The APT allows multiple sources of systematic risk. Arbitrage in expectations: if a security deviates from the SML, investors exploit the mispricing by going long the underpriced asset and shorting a same-beta portfolio. Multi-factor model: $E[R_i] = R_f + \sum_k \beta_{i,k}\lambda_k$. Chen, Roll, and Ross identified macro factors: inflation, GNP, investor confidence, yield curve shifts.

Chapter VII: Where Do Betas Come From?

Core Topics

Beta estimation via regression of security returns on market returns (typically 60 months). Leverage increases equity beta: $\beta_{\text{equity}} = \beta_{\text{asset}} \times (D+E)/E$. Unlevering to find asset beta from comparable firms, then relevering to your own capital structure. Application to project valuation, M&A, and capital budgeting -- each project should use its own beta, not the company-wide cost of capital.

Unlevering / Relevering

$$\beta_{\text{asset}} = \frac{E}{D+E}\,\beta_{\text{equity}} \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \beta_{\text{equity}} = \beta_{\text{asset}} \times \frac{D+E}{E}$$

Chapter VIII: Information and Market Efficiency

Core Topics

Market efficiency: how rapidly information embeds in prices. Three forms: Weak (past prices), Semi-Strong (all public info), Strong (all info including private). Evidence: event studies show rapid adjustment; professional managers show no consistent outperformance; daily momentum exists but is not profitably exploitable. Random walk approximation holds for liquid markets. Efficient markets benefit investors through price stability, reduced search costs, and liquidity.

Chapter IX: Long-Term Corporate Financing

Core Topics

Two primary approaches: Selling ownership (stocks, warrants) and Borrowing (bonds, preferred shares). The life cycle of stock: design, IPO, exchange trading. Bond features: coupon, maturity, covenants, callability, sinking funds. Preferred shares: non-voting, senior to equity, fixed dividends. Warrants: right to purchase shares at a fixed price over a fixed period.
