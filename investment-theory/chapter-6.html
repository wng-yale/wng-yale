Chapter VI

The Arbitrage Pricing Theory

Stephen Ross's multi-factor alternative to the CAPM, and the role of arbitrage in pricing securities.

Beyond a Single Factor

The Arbitrage Pricing Theory (APT), developed by Stephen Ross, doesn't require all investors to hold identical portfolios or rely on a single market portfolio. Instead, it assumes multiple systematic risk factors drive security returns.

Arbitrage in Expectations

When securities deviate from their theoretically correct prices, investors exploit these mispricings through what Ross called "arbitrage in expectations" — locking in positive expected payoffs by simultaneously buying underpriced and shorting overpriced securities with equivalent risk profiles.

Unlike true arbitrage with zero risk, APT arbitrage involves risk because actual returns may diverge from expectations due to unexpected factor movements.

Identifying Factors

The APT framework uses macro-economic surprises as risk factors:

  • Unexpected changes in inflation
  • Surprises in GNP growth
  • Unexpected interest rate movements
  • Shifts in investor confidence

Portfolio Customization

Unlike the CAPM's one-size-fits-all prescription, the APT allows investors to tailor exposures to specific risk factors and perform scenario analysis under different economic conditions.

Strengths and Challenges

The APT is more flexible than the CAPM and accommodates multiple risk sources. However, it requires investors to accurately perceive and estimate factor sensitivities, and disagreement persists on identifying the "true" risk factors.
