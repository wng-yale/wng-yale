Chapter V

Further Explorations of the Capital Asset Pricing Model

The Security Market Line, beta estimation, expectations vs. realizations, and the empirical challenges to the CAPM.

The Risk-Return Tradeoff

Stephen Ross provided a formal argument for CAPM pricing. When an investor considers adding a small quantity of asset A to the market portfolio, the fundamental relationship emerges: beta (β = cov(A,m)/var(m)) determines expected returns.

The CAPM Equation

E[Ri] = Rf + βi[E(Rm) − Rf]

Critically, this model excludes individual security volatility from the discount rate calculation, focusing instead on systematic risk via beta.

The Security Market Line

A linear relationship between risk (measured by beta) and expected return. This linearity prevents arbitrage opportunities — any curved relationship would allow investors to exploit differences through borrowing and leveraging.

Expectations vs. Realizations

Realized returns deviate from theoretical predictions due to random error:

Ri = Rf + βi[Rm − Rf] + ei

This explains why historical data rarely matches CAPM predictions precisely.

A Practical Example

Consider a pharmaceutical investment with β = 0.5, Rf = 5%, E[Rm] = 13.5%. The required return is 9.25% — independent of the project's 75% total volatility. Only systematic risk matters.

Beta Estimation in Practice

Analysts regress 60 months of security returns against market index returns (typically the S&P 500), though this substitutes a proxy for the theoretical world market portfolio.

Empirical Challenges

Fama and French demonstrated that beta alone inadequately explains portfolio returns. Additional factors — book-to-market ratios, earnings-price ratios, and company size — proved significant, leading researchers toward multi-factor models.
