Chapter IV

The Portfolio Approach to Risk

The Capital Asset Pricing Model, the tangency portfolio, and why all investors want to hold the same portfolio of risky assets.

The Tangency Portfolio Discovery

In the 1960s, financial researchers building on Markowitz's work realized a profound implication: if all investors can borrow and lend at a riskless rate, all investors would want to hold precisely the same portfolio of risky assets — the tangency portfolio. This is the Two Fund Separation Theorem.

The Capital Asset Pricing Model

William Sharpe and others developed the CAPM to identify the tangency portfolio. The model operates under key assumptions including perfect asset divisibility, unanimous investor agreement on inputs, and a two-period economy.

The Birthday Cake Proof

The core CAPM argument uses an elegant metaphor: the tangency portfolio will be a value-weighted mix of all assets in the world. Imagine a cake sliced horizontally by company size (market capitalization) and vertically by investor wealth. Each investor naturally holds assets weighted by their proportion of global wealth:

weight on asset i = (price × shares) / world wealth

Implications for Investing

The CAPM suggests that the S&P 500 — already a capital-weighted portfolio — approximates the optimal tangency portfolio. This legitimized index fund strategies emphasizing low-cost, buy-and-hold approaches requiring minimal trading.

Critical Limitations

Markets aren't always in equilibrium, investors don't hold truly global portfolios, and human capital isn't tradable. Yet the CAPM remains valuable both as an investment prescription and as a framework for understanding how securities should be priced based on their contribution to portfolio risk.
