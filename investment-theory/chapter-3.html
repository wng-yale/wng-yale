Chapter III

Preferences and Investor Choice

How investors select a single portfolio from the efficient frontier by expressing their preferences in risk-return space.

Iso-Utility Curves and Risk Aversion

Iso-utility curves are mathematical representations of investor preferences in risk-return space. More risk-averse investors demand sharply higher returns as volatility increases, while risk-tolerant investors accept lower compensation. Portfolio optimization finds the tangency point between these curves and the efficient frontier.

The Shortfall Criterion

Rather than mapping complete preference structures, investors can specify a floor return below which they wish to avoid falling. The model calculates:

Shortfall Ratio = (Expected Return − Floor) / Standard Deviation

The portfolio maximizing this ratio has the highest probability of exceeding the floor.

The Sharpe Ratio

When the risk-free rate serves as the floor, the slope of the shortfall line is called the Sharpe Ratio. This identifies the portfolio most likely to outperform Treasury bills and has become one of the most widely used performance measures in finance.

Value at Risk (VaR)

VaR estimates potential losses over a given horizon:

RVaR = Rp − T × STDp

where T represents the t-statistic for a specified confidence interval. For example, a $100 million pension portfolio might have a monthly VaR of $5.4 million at the 95% confidence level.

Practical Limitations

Utility function estimation is theoretically sound but practically difficult. Safety-first methodology offers a more intuitive alternative by focusing on specific probability thresholds rather than complete preference mapping.
