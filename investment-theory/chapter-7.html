Chapter VII

Where Do Betas Come From?

Leverage, asset vs. equity betas, and practical applications in project valuation and M&A analysis.

Beta as Systematic Risk

Beta quantifies exposure to market-wide risk — not diversifiable risk. High-technology firms exhibit elevated betas during expansions but struggle in contractions, while the food industry is a low-beta sector due to stable demand regardless of economic conditions.

Practical Beta Estimation

Beta is calculated through linear regression comparing historical security returns against market returns. The slope indicates sensitivity: a company with β = 1.5 will theoretically experience 30% returns when markets rise 20%. However, betas change over time and the model may omit relevant risk factors.

The Critical Role of Leverage

Financial leverage amplifies systematic risk dramatically. Consider a cable television company:

  • Asset beta (unlevered): 0.67
  • Equity beta (levered): 1.85
  • Expected return rises from 11.39% to 21.41% due to debt financing
βasset = (Equity weight × βequity) + (Debt weight × βdebt)

with debt beta typically assumed to be zero.

Four Practical Applications

  1. Project Valuation: Using industry-comparable asset betas to discount cash flows for new ventures
  2. M&A Analysis: Evaluating acquisition targets using estimated betas and growth rates
  3. P/E Ratios: Cross-checking discount rates against earnings multiples
  4. Capital Budgeting: Allocating project-specific discount rates rather than company-wide rates
