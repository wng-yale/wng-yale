Chapter VIII

Information and the Efficiency of Capital Markets

How rapidly and completely asset prices incorporate relevant information — the three forms of market efficiency.

What Is Market Efficiency?

Market efficiency reflects Adam Smith's "invisible hand" — arbitrageurs exploiting mispricings drive prices toward fair economic value. The question is how rapidly and completely this process occurs.

Weak-Form Efficiency

Past price patterns cannot generate excess profits. While daily momentum exists in the data, trading costs typically eliminate profits from exploiting these patterns. Technical analysts who rely solely on price charts face a formidable empirical challenge.

Semi-Strong Form Efficiency

All publicly available information is reflected in prices. This approximates reality in U.S. equity markets, though some backtested strategies — dividend yield screening, earnings surprises — show historical profitability. The key question: do these profits persist out of sample?

Strong-Form Efficiency

Even private information is impounded in prices. Merger case studies reveal a "tendency" toward this form through pre-announcement price movements, though the public announcement typically still causes price reactions.

Key Evidence

Event studies show stock prices react within minutes to major corporate news. Discount rate announcements are reflected in prices the same day — not the next day. This speed of adjustment is the hallmark of an efficient market.

Practical Implications

  • Accounting tricks cannot fool efficient markets for long
  • Corporate managers cannot systematically mislead investors about asset values
  • Arbitrageurs provide beneficial liquidity and keep prices near fundamental values
  • Information that is easier to obtain is more likely already priced in
What if everyone followed the same strategy? Wouldn't the opportunity go away?

Market efficiency remains "a good first approximation" for liquid, free markets.
