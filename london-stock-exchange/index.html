The London Stock Exchange, 1869–1929

The Investor’s Monthly Manual recorded the world’s largest securities market month by month for six decades. From its hand-collected record we reconstruct the London market — its return, its risk premium, and how the reward for bearing risk fell as you climbed the capital structure from common stock to preferred shares to bonds. The research

Capital Structure, Seniority, and Risk Premia

Evidence from the London Stock Exchange, 1870–1929

Using security-level data from the Investor’s Monthly Manual, we build capital-weighted return indexes for the London market and measure the reward for risk at each level of the capital structure. We find a persistent equity risk premium of about 3.7% over commercial paper and 4.5% over long-term government bonds, and returns that decline monotonically with seniority — common stocks earned more than preferred shares, which earned more than corporate bonds. The rolling ten-year stock-minus-bond spread was positive in every interval of the sixty-year sample. Most of the total return came from income, not price appreciation, as the explorer below makes plain.

The structure of the news

The Structure of London Financial News

A companion project reads the London financial press — the Investor’s Monthly Manual and The Statist — end to end, using topic models and diachronic word embeddings to trace how reporting shifted from terse price records to narrative-driven coverage over roughly 150 years.

News and prices are linked. In the companion Financial Times series, the mix of news topics forecasts the market: commodity, labour, and foreign coverage predict higher future volatility, while routine corporate and dividend coverage predicts calm — explaining as much as ~22% of forward volatility in the 1931–45 era.

Governance & geography · exploratory

Who ran these companies, and where? From the December annual issues of the IMM we extract each firm’s chairman, secretary, and registered office, then geocode the addresses. The map below places roughly 370 City of London companies in 1890, coloured by sector — a first look at the physical geography of Victorian finance. Extending this to board interlocks and directorial dynasties across 1880–1929 is work in progress.
