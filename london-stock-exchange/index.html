The London Stock Exchange, 1869–1929

The Investor’s Monthly Manual recorded the world’s largest securities market month by month for six decades. From its hand-collected record we reconstruct the London equity market — its return, its risk premium, and how much of that return came from dividends rather than price appreciation. The research

Risk Premiums in the London Market, 1870–1929

William N. Goetzmann, Fernando Reyes De La Luz & K. Geert Rouwenhorst — chapter forthcoming in the Ibbotson volume; extended version circulated as an NBER working paper, “Capital Structure, Seniority, and Risk Premia: Evidence from the London Stock Exchange.”

Using the Investor’s Monthly Manual, we measure the realized equity risk premium on the London market and decompose returns across the capital structure and across domestic versus overseas securities. The cap-weighted equity premium over the risk-free rate averaged roughly 2–3% a year — and, strikingly for the era, most of the total return came from dividends, not price appreciation, as the explorer below makes plain.
